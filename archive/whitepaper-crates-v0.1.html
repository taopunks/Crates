01Abstract

Infinite listings are worth nothing

Every NFT marketplace made the same choice: list everything, curate nothing. The result is a market where the signal-to-noise ratio rounds to zero and where being “on the marketplace” carries no information at all. Bittensor made the opposite choice for machine intelligence — a hard cap of 128 subnet slots, priced by demand, pruned by performance — and the seat itself became one of the most contested assets in crypto. Crates apply that discipline to agent-bearing NFT collections.

The thesis

A Crate is a numbered registry seat — #001 … #128 — that binds a collection into the 8004 protocol: its contracts, its metadata, its markets, its provenance ledger, and above all its agents. On 8004, an NFT is not a picture; it is an ERC-8004 economic actor with an identity, a reputation, a token-bound account, and a work history. A Crate is the container that makes a set of those actors legible, comparable, and scarce.

Why scarcity is the product

Curation without cost is opinion. Curation with a hard cap is allocation — every admitted collection displaces a potential rival, so admission itself carries information. Bittensor demonstrated the full mechanism: a capped slot count, a registration lock that doubles on each claim and decays between claims, and deregistration that recycles the weakest seat. Demand for those slots pushed registration costs to four-figure TAO sums by 2026. None of that value came from the slot doing anything — it came from the slot being one of 128.

What Crates deliberately are not. No mining. No hashpower. No emissions schedule. No crate tokens. The measurable substance inside a Crate is commerce and agent work that already settles on chain 964 — TAO is the only money in the system, and the ledger is the only oracle.

02The Primitive

What a Crate is

A Crate is not a listing. A listing is a row anyone can add; a Crate is a seat — numbered, scarce, stateful, and eventually ownable. Each Crate binds five things:

1 · The Manifest
Identity of the cargo: NFT contract(s), supply, token-id scheme, render mode (raster, procedural-3D wireframe, remote), category, verification. Today a registry row; tomorrow on-chain metadata.
2 · The Agent Surface
Every token in a crate can register as an ERC-8004 agent — identity, reputation, validation, and an ERC-6551 token-bound account that earns and holds TAO. The crate is the census tract for its agents.
3 · The Market Venue
Native, audited trading (TunkMarket on chain 964) with contract-verified buys — the displayed price is always re-read from the chain at click time. Own-venue collections keep their own markets, honestly labeled.
4 · The Provenance Ledger
Every token’s history from genesis mint through every sale, backfilled from the chain archive and exposed transaction-by-transaction. The volume a crate displays is the sum of rows anyone can audit.

5 · The Score (future). The measurable output of everything above: settled agent tasks, TAO earned to token-bound accounts, token-verified volume, active agents, distinct holders. One number per crate, computed from chain facts only — the analog of subnet emissions, without a single miner.

Curation with teeth — already demonstrated

The registry has already said no. Two candidate collections (Chunkies, Taoist) were surveyed, scoped, and then withdrawn when their launchpad collapsed and the teams’ commitment failed the bar — their seats were reclaimed before ever going live. A registry that cannot reject cannot curate; Crates rejected before they scaled. That precedent is the cultural core of the system.

03The Number

Why 128 seats

128 is not aesthetic. It is the number Bittensor itself converged on for subnet slots — large enough for a real economy of specialization, small enough that every seat stays legible and every admission is contested. The parallel is worth stating precisely, because Crates borrow the mechanism while discarding the machinery.

Bittensor Subnets8004 Crates
THE SEATNetuid, capped at 128 active slots (expanding toward 256)Crate number, capped at 128 (#001–#128)
THE CARGOA market for machine intelligence (miners + validators)A collection of ERC-8004 agents (NFTs with identity, capital, work)
ADMISSIONTAO lock; cost doubles per registration, decays over time; refundable on deregistrationPhase 2: same curve — lock, double, decay, refund on exit (§06)
MEASUREMENTEmissions from validator consensus over miner workCrateScore from settled tasks, verified volume, agent activity (§07)

WHAT’S DELETEDMining, hashrate, validator hardware, emission schedules, per-seat tokens— all of it. The chain already settles the work; Crates only measure it.

The scarcity arithmetic

OpenSea hosts millions of collections; the top marketplace signal a collection can earn there is a checkmark. Under a 128-cap, the weakest admitted crate is still one of 128 — top 0.01% of the category by construction. When admission also locks capital that could be reclaimed by a stronger entrant, holding a seat becomes a continuous claim: this cargo is still worth its slot.

Levelling honestly: the cap is a governance choice, not a law of nature — Bittensor is already expanding 128 → 256 under demand. Crates should treat 128 as the constitution’s starting article: changeable, but only loudly, slowly, and with the existing seat-holders’ interests priced in.

The seat as an asset

In Phase 1 the CrateRegistry mints each seat as an ERC-721 — 128 crate tokens, ever. Owning CRATE #007 is the right to bind and configure its manifest, subject to protocol rules. Seats transfer like any NFT; the registry doesn’t care who curates, only that the cargo performs. This mirrors subnet ownership — and quietly makes the crates themselves the platform’s first native scarce asset without issuing a single fungible token.

05The Path

Four phases to a subnet-grade registry

Each phase ships something usable and reversible before the next hardens it. The platform has run this exact playbook once already — agent registration went from an idea to a production, guardrailed, on-chain flow — and Crates follow the same arc: centralize to learn, decentralize what survived.

P0 · LIVE
Curated register (today)
Editorial admission, hard 128 cap honored from day one, chain-true measurement of everything inside. Purpose: prove the container, the indexers, and the culture of rejection. Status: 3 seats filled, 2 rejections already on the record.
P1
CrateRegistry on chain 964
A single audited contract mints the 128 seats as ERC-721s and stores each crate’s manifest (cargo contract, supply, render hints, venue). The app becomes a reader of a registry it no longer owns — the same inversion the agent side already made. Genesis allocation: existing seats honored; a majority of seats held in a timelocked reserve so P2 economics — not favoritism — distribute them.
P2
Economic admission — the lock curve
Permissionless seat claims priced by the subnet mechanism: a TAO lock (not burn) that doubles on each successful claim and decays toward a floor between claims; refundable on voluntary exit. When all 128 are bound, the lowest-scoring crate past its immunity window becomes reclaimable — its lock returns, its history remains. Registration revenue model mirrors the platform’s existing precedent (fee-flip after traction, e.g. the τ0.01 agent-registration flip at 100 registrations).
P3
The Cratergraph — measuring agents across all crates
The cross-crate metagraph: every ERC-8004 agent in every crate scored on the same chain-derived axes (settled tasks, TAO earned to its TBA, uptime of mandates, market activity), rolled up into per-crate CrateScores each epoch. One leaderboard, 128 rows, zero oracles — every input is a row in a ledger the platform already indexes. This is the payload of the whole system: comparable agents, comparable collections, on public data.
P4
Mechanisms (optional, earned)
Per-crate hooks once P3 data proves demand: crate treasuries fed by venue fees, launch mechanics for SEALED seats, score-weighted discovery placement. Explicitly out of scope unless earned: per-crate tokens, emissions, anything that reintroduces the machinery Crates exist to delete.

The design covenant: at every phase, the numbers users see must remain reconstructable from chain 964 by a third party with no access to 8004 Labs. Decentralizing the registry is Phase 1–2; the measurement was born decentralized.

06Economics

The lock curve & seat lifecycle

Phase 2 admission borrows Bittensor’s battle-tested pricing verbatim, because it solved exactly this problem: pricing a fixed set of seats under unknown demand, without an auctioneer.

// seat pricing (per successful claim) lock_cost(t) = max( FLOOR, last_lock_cost × 2 × decay(t − t_last_claim) ) decay(Δt) = halves linearly over DECAY_INTERVAL (cost falls back toward FLOOR when nobody claims) on_claim : TAO locked in CrateRegistry (not burned — escrowed) on_exit : lock returned, seat reopens at current curve price on_reclaim : lowest CrateScore past IMMUNITY forfeits the seat, lock returned, manifest archived, history immutable
  • Lock, not burn. Capital is committed, not destroyed — the seat-holder’s stake is recoverable by exiting or losing the seat, which keeps the claim honest without being punitive.
  • Doubling + decay finds the market price with no governance in the loop: hot demand compounds cost geometrically; cold periods let it fall back toward the floor.
  • Immunity for new cargo. A freshly bound crate cannot be reclaimed during its immunity window — new collections get time to breathe before the score compares them to incumbents (the subnet deregistration lesson, imported wholesale).

The no-token covenant, restated. Crates issue no fungible token at any phase. TAO is the money; the crate seat (an NFT, 128 ever) is the only new scarce asset, and its value derives from the right to curate — not from a promise of cash flows. If the ecosystem ever deserves a tradable index of crate activity, Bittensor’s dTAO already demonstrates the native path — but that is a bridge for a different decade of this project, and it is not promised here.

07Measurement

CrateScore: proof of commerce, not proof of work

Subnets need miners because intelligence has to be generated before it can be measured. Crate cargo is different: agents and collections already generate measurable, settled activity on chain 964. The score doesn’t create work — it reads it. Every proposed input below is already indexed in production by the Phase-0 platform.

Gaming, honestly confronted

  • Wash trading → token-verification helps but does not cure self-dealing; mitigations: volume contributes with diminishing weight per counterparty, and fee-bearing venues make wash cycles cost real TAO.
  • Sybil agents → registration fees per agent (the existing fee-flip) price out mass fake identities; task-settlement weight requires a counterparty who paid escrow.
  • Score oracles → there are none. The score spec must be a pure function of public chain state, published with the code, reproducible by anyone. Disagreement becomes a pull request, not a dispute.

Why this beats emissions for this domain: subnet emissions answer “who computed best?” — a question only validators can judge. CrateScore answers “whose cargo did real, paid, on-chain work?” — a question the ledger already answered. Deleting miners isn’t a compromise; for property registries it is the correct architecture.

08Risks · Roadmap · Record

What could kill it, and what’s next

Named risks

  • Centralization overhang (now). Until P1 ships, 8004 Labs is a trusted curator. Mitigation: publish this covenant, keep every measurement reproducible, ship P1 before selling any seat.
  • Empty-cap theater. 3/128 seats filled means scarcity is a promise, not yet a price. Mitigation: the cap only starts pricing at P2 — until then it is honesty about intent, and admission stays free but earned.
  • Score capture. Any composite score invites optimization of the metric over the mission. Mitigation: pure-function transparency, slow parameter change, and the cultural precedent that seats have already been withdrawn for non-performance.
  • Regulatory surface. Seats are utility NFTs conferring curation rights — no fungible issuance, no revenue promise, locks refundable. Keep it that way.
